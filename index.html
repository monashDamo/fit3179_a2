From production to the lightbulb

Australian Energy Explained (1995-2022)

by Damanpreet Singh

There is a lot which happens behind the scenes which enables us in australia to use our electricity. From the different power plants which generate our electricity, and deciding where to build new ones. To where our energy actually goes. And what part renewables play in this.

The purpose of this visualization is to explain plainly the relationship between Australia and its electricity, where it comes from, how its used, and where it is heading in the future.

Enjoy!

Some background information

Energy in Australia is regulated and controlled by three main bodies. These are the Australian Energy Regulator (AER), the Australian Energy Market Commission (AEMC) and finally the Australian Energy Market Operator (AEMO). The AER is responsible for regulating the wholesale energy and gas markets in Australia. The AEMC is responsible for creating rule and regulations which are enforced by the AER. And finally the AEMO. This is responsible for overseeing various functions of the australian gas and energy markets. These can basically be summed up to maintaining and overseeing these markets, managing these markets, and finally leading the design of the future energy systems in all of australia.

Whilst all 3 of these bodies are equally important, we will mostly be focusing on the AEMO.

We will also be using MegaWatt (MW) and PetaJoule (PJ) energy measurements in this visualization.

1. Where does our energy come from?

The graph on the left shows where our energy come from (2). As we can see, most of energy is produced through coal (brown and black coal). And some of it is imported. But most of it comes from non-renewable sources. Only a small percentage of it comes from renewable sources, around 2.66%. All in all, just considering 2022-23 australia generated roughly 20,836 PetaJoules worth of energy!

But with that said, how do we actually make the energy we get inhouse? Using power plants!

This "Power Stations over Australia" (3) graph shows the distribution of power plants across australia. As you can most of the coal plants (click on the legend) are placed on the outskirts of australia, as coal is found in rugged terrains in basins and underground deposits. On the other hand, solar farms are placed in the middle of australia and near victoria. Wind farms, on the other hand are also placed on the outskirts as well, since it receives good amount of wind. Ofcourse for all of these options, these farms also need to be near civilisation as well to provide them with electricity.

Even though there are ample amount of renewable plants, non-renewable plants generate far more power. This can be seen on the "Distribution of power plants over fuel type" (3) graph. If you adjust the minimum nameplate capacity, you will see that many renewable plants will just disappear. By 200 capacity (MW), wind and water are the only remaining renewable sources. By 500, water is the only one. But the best water plant doesn't generate as much power as the best non-renewable plant. This being the Eraring Coal Plant which has a capacity of 2,880 MW.

2. Where does our energy go?

As I explained, most of our energy is produced in australia. But once it is produced, where does it go?

Our energy production can basically be summed by as non-renewable, imports and renewable. On the other hand, our energy usage can be summed up as being exported, used in various industries and also incurring some loss. "Power Production in Australia (2022-23)" already showed us how much energy is produced. But how does it get used?

From the "Consumption of energy (PJ) in various industries" (2), we can see that no renewable energy is used in any of these industries. Black coal is used primarily to produce electricity. On the other hand Diesel is used a lot, mostly in the transport industry, then being scattered around Agriculture, Water and Waste, Construction, and more.

You might be confused as to where "Petrol", "Diesel" even come from, since we didn't see them in the first section. Diesel and Petrol are simply byproducts of Crude Oil which we did look at before. They are actually produced largely in the manufacturing industry, through the oil refinement process. All of this energy together comes to about 5,467 PJ. However that there something very important we need to note, which is that Electricity is basically creating electricity to THEN be later used in other industries. This is indicated by the electricity also being a fuel source for many industries. Since this is not explicitily an industry, we need to remove this. Doing so we get some net usage of around 3,863 PJ

Earlier we saw said that we generated around 18,709 PJ worth of energy? So where is the rest going? Its simply being exported.

Around 15 thousand (PJ) was exported in (2022-23). And of that most of what coal, followed by natural gases, and then various other fuels.

You might have noticed that renewable energy doesn't get used much, atleast in the various industries. Its mainly used to generate electricity, used in manufacturing and residential areas.

But it doesn't see much use, but will this ever change?

3. Looking to the future

According to AEMO, Australia plans to transition the NEM network to net zero by 25 years. There plan include to drastically increase renewable capabilities. This includes increasing battery storage to 49 GW to 3 GW, increase Wind and Solar plants capacities to 127 GW, and stop electricity generation from coal to 0%. So are we on our way to meeting this target?

If we look at this graph (2), we can see that we have made ample progress. Whilst most of our energy is still generated by non-renewables. This might look grim, but our plan is to turn to renewables for electricity for atleast the NEM network by 2050. So are we on the right track?

If we consider the above graph (1). You might have noticed that a lot of power plants are planned to get shut down, and most of those actually include renewables. That may seem a bit counterintuitive, but that is simply because this factors in the lifespan of these plants, these being solar, wind. The important thing to realize is that so many new renewable plants have sprung up quite recently near the 2020s. This sentiment is similarily echoed in the "Amount of electricity (PJ) by year" graph which shows that we rely on more renewables for our electricity needs than ever before.

Its also very important to highlight that not many non-renewable plants have an expected date listed, but that is infact accurate, as the AEMO roadmap simply highlights moving to renewables for our electrical needs, not going completely green. With that said, the progress we have seen so far is very much admirable.
